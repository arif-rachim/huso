üåü The Chef Who Revolutionized Chemistry

Picture this: It's 1772, and 29-year-old Antoine Lavoisier is about to completely revolutionize chemistry with a simple but brilliant idea. At the time, most chemists believed in "phlogiston" - an invisible substance that was supposedly released during burning. But Lavoisier had a radical thought: "What if I actually weigh everything before and after reactions?"

In his elegant Paris laboratory, Lavoisier began conducting meticulous experiments. He would place materials in sealed glass vessels and weigh them before heating. After reactions occurred, he would weigh everything again - including any gases produced! To everyone's amazement, the total mass never changed. Not even by a single gram!

This led to his revolutionary insight: "Nothing is lost, nothing is created, everything is transformed." He had discovered the Law of Conservation of Mass! But even more importantly, he realized that chemical reactions follow precise mathematical relationships - like recipes in a cookbook.

Lavoisier showed that when you burn 32 grams of sulfur with 32 grams of oxygen, you always get exactly 64 grams of sulfur dioxide. Not 63 grams, not 65 grams - exactly 64! He discovered that chemistry follows exact mathematical rules, which we now call stoichiometry (literally "measuring elements").

Tragically, Lavoisier was executed during the French Revolution in 1794. The judge reportedly said, "The Republic has no need for scientists." But his mathematical approach to chemistry survived and became the foundation of modern chemical science. Today, every time you balance a chemical equation or calculate how much product a reaction will make, you're using Lavoisier's revolutionary insight that chemistry is fundamentally mathematical!

ü•™ The Sandwich Analogy

üîç Finding the Limiting Reactant

Method: Calculate how much product each reactant can make

Limiting reactant: The one that makes the LEAST product

Excess reactant: The one with leftover material

Theoretical yield: Maximum product possible from limiting reactant
